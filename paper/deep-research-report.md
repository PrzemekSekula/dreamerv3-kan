## World models and the Dreamer lineage

Modern world-model reinforcement learning is usually traced to *World Models*, which showed that agents can learn compact generative models of environments and even train policies inside learned “dreams.” PlaNet sharpened this direction by introducing latent dynamics planning with a recurrent state-space model that combines deterministic and stochastic latent components for long-horizon prediction. Dreamer then moved behavior learning fully into latent imagination, showing that policies and values can be optimized from imagined trajectories rather than only with planning at decision time. citeturn36search0turn22search2turn22search0

The Dreamer line is especially important for your paper because it progressively made latent world models both more expressive and more practical. DreamerV2 introduced **discrete** latent representations and showed that behaviors could be learned inside a separately trained world model strongly enough to reach human-level Atari performance. DreamerV3 then reframed the method as a more general cross-domain algorithm: its world model encodes sensory inputs into **discrete representations**, and its actor and critic learn from imagined latent trajectories predicted by that world model. In the DreamerV3 description, the non-recurrent heads for vector prediction are still MLP-based, which makes your decision to target the actor-critic module architecturally natural rather than ad hoc. citeturn35view0turn42view0turn7view3

The world-model context also explains why your computational argument matters. Dreamer-style agents repeatedly evaluate the policy and value networks during imagination rollouts, so even moderate per-layer overhead can multiply quickly. The Atari 100k benchmark, introduced in the SimPLe line, was designed around only 100k agent steps, corresponding to 400k frames with the usual action repeat, and has become a standard low-data testbed for measuring sample efficiency on 26 Atari games. In that regime, a KAN substitution is not just a representational choice; it is a systems choice that can help or hurt both throughput and final learning quality. citeturn41search1turn41search14turn42view0

## KANs, efficient variants, and mixed empirical evidence

KANs, introduced by entity["people","Ziming Liu","mit researcher"] and collaborators, replace fixed node activations and scalar weights with learnable univariate functions on edges, typically parameterized by splines. The original paper framed KANs as more interpretable than MLPs and argued that spline locality could provide local plasticity; it also included a toy continual-learning example in which KANs avoided catastrophic forgetting better than MLPs in a simple one-dimensional setting. Those claims are directly relevant to your paper’s motivation around interpretability and forgetting. citeturn27view0turn28view1turn28view2

At the same time, the post-2024 KAN literature has been dominated by attempts to address computational overhead. FastKAN showed that the cubic B-splines used in the original KAN can be well approximated by Gaussian radial basis functions, yielding a much faster implementation. ReLU-KAN proposed a formulation based on matrix addition, dot products, and ReLU to improve GPU efficiency and reported a large speedup over classical KAN. UKAN addressed bounded-grid limitations and introduced an accelerated library that reduces the complexity of basis-spline evaluation while allowing unbounded inputs. Efficient-KAN, a widely used software implementation rather than a peer-reviewed paper, further reduced memory cost by reformulating KAN computation as basis activation followed by linear combination, turning the forward pass into straightforward matrix multiplication. citeturn30view0turn21search1turn21search2turn31view0

This matters for how you frame your own contribution. In the broader KAN literature, “making KAN practical” is **not new**. What is new in your manuscript is not the general idea that KANs need architectural simplification, but the much more specific claim that Dreamer’s discrete one-hot latent structure creates an **exact first-layer simplification**. That is a more precise and stronger contribution than a general efficiency claim, and I would recommend leaning into that specificity. citeturn30view0turn31view0turn37search1turn38view1

It is also important to reflect the current mixed empirical picture. *KAN or MLP: A Fairer Comparison* found that, under parameter- and FLOP-controlled comparisons, MLPs generally outperform KANs outside symbolic formula representation tasks, and it reported worse forgetting for KANs than MLPs in a standard class-incremental setting. A later dedicated study on catastrophic forgetting in KANs refined that picture further: KANs can retain information well in low-dimensional algorithmic settings, but remain vulnerable in higher-dimensional domains such as image classification and language modeling. So the literature supports your use of KANs as an **interesting architectural hypothesis**, but not as a settled superior replacement for MLPs in high-dimensional RL. citeturn26view0turn32view0

A final nuance that helps your paper is that hybrid KAN/MLP designs are already becoming common outside RL. Time-series benchmarking found that KAN performance was often driven largely by the base component, and that hybrid structures such as `MLP_KAN` could improve robustness. Other hybrid proposals, including MLP-KAN and MJKAN, explicitly combine KAN-style function learning with MLP-style representation learning or affine modulation to recover practical efficiency. Those papers do not make your Dreamer-specific binary-equivalence argument, but they do support the broader idea that **pure KAN substitutions are often less compelling than carefully chosen hybrids**. citeturn39view0turn38view1turn37search1

## KANs in reinforcement learning before world models

Before KANs appeared inside Dreamer-like systems, they had already entered the RL literature as alternative function approximators. In online RL, a 2024 study evaluated KAN-based PPO on DeepMind Control proprioceptive tasks and reported performance comparable to MLP-based PPO, often with fewer parameters. In offline RL, a separate study replaced the actor and critic networks of conservative Q-learning on D4RL with KANs and found that KAN could achieve performance close to MLPs with significantly fewer parameters. These papers are important because they show that KANs were already being used seriously inside policy and value networks before any Dreamer-specific integration was attempted. citeturn24search0turn24search7

Other RL applications broaden that picture. A load-balancing paper used PPO with a one-layer KAN actor and an MLP critic to learn interpretable network-control policies. A human-in-the-loop feature selection paper built a DDQN whose Q-network and target network were both KANs, arguing for higher interpretability and compactness. Taken together, these works make it hard to claim novelty for “KANs in RL” or even for “KANs in actor-critic style networks.” The novelty has to come from the **world-model setting**, the **Dreamer architecture**, the **discrete latent structure**, or the **exact mathematical simplification** you prove. citeturn40view0turn25view0

That framing actually helps your paper. The gap in the literature is not whether KANs can be used in reinforcement learning at all. The gap is whether they can be used **efficiently inside imagination-based world-model RL**, where networks are evaluated many times per rollout and where Dreamer’s latent state has structural properties that ordinary model-free RL policies do not. That is the niche your manuscript is best suited to occupy. citeturn42view0turn24search0turn24search7

## Ready-to-adapt literature review

World models have become a central paradigm for sample-efficient reinforcement learning because they allow policies to improve from imagined trajectories rather than only from direct environment interaction. Early work on World Models showed that compact generative representations could support policy learning inside learned simulations, and PlaNet made this idea substantially more practical by introducing recurrent state-space models with both deterministic and stochastic latent components. Dreamer then demonstrated that actor and critic networks can be trained directly from latent imagination, and later versions of the framework strengthened both the representational and optimization machinery. In particular, DreamerV2 introduced discrete latent representations for Atari-scale control, and DreamerV3 established a robust, general-purpose formulation in which the world model encodes observations into discrete latent states and the actor-critic learns purely from imagined latent rollouts. This lineage makes DreamerV3 a natural substrate for studying alternative function approximators in the behavior module. citeturn36search0turn22search2turn22search0turn35view0turn42view0

In parallel, Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks have emerged as an alternative to MLPs by replacing fixed node activations and scalar edge weights with learnable univariate functions, usually parameterized by splines. The original KAN work argued that this design can improve interpretability and local plasticity, and it motivated a broad wave of follow-up work on efficient implementations and hybrid variants. FastKAN approximates spline bases with Gaussian radial basis functions, ReLU-KAN uses matrix-friendly ReLU constructions, UKAN removes bounded-grid limitations, and Efficient-KAN reformulates the computation to reduce memory overhead. At the same time, the empirical picture remains mixed: fair comparisons have often found that KANs do not consistently outperform well-matched MLPs outside narrow settings, and later continual-learning studies suggest that the claimed forgetting advantages of KANs diminish in higher-dimensional domains. These results imply that KANs should be treated less as universally superior replacements for MLPs and more as structured inductive biases whose usefulness depends strongly on task geometry and architectural context. citeturn27view0turn28view1turn30view0turn21search1turn21search2turn31view0turn26view0turn32view0

A smaller but growing literature has already applied KANs to reinforcement learning. Prior work has evaluated KAN-based PPO in online control, KAN-based actor-critic networks in offline conservative Q-learning, and KANs in value-based settings such as DDQN, often emphasizing compactness or interpretability rather than raw performance gains. The closest work to the present study is KAN-Dreamer, which integrates KAN and FastKAN components into DreamerV3 and studies replacements in perception, latent prediction, and behavior learning. However, that work frames feasibility primarily through vectorization, fixed-grid approximation, and JAX-compatible implementation choices. By contrast, the present paper targets a more specific structural property of Dreamer-style world models: the stochastic RSSM state is represented by binary one-hot latents, implying that a KAN input layer operating on that discrete representation cannot exploit nontrivial spline behavior at the input stage. This motivates a hybrid design in which the first transformation is replaced by a standard dense affine map and KAN layers are reserved for subsequent continuous hidden representations, where their functional flexibility can actually be used. citeturn24search0turn24search7turn25view0turn40view0turn11view0turn14view1turn14view2

Against this background, your paper’s strongest contribution is not simply “KANs inside DreamerV3,” because that has already been explored, but rather a **principled architectural argument** for where KANs should and should not be used inside a Dreamer-style behavior module. Framed this way, the paper contributes to the literature on hybrid KAN architectures, efficient world-model RL, and discrete latent policy learning simultaneously: it narrows the gap between generic KAN efficiency tricks and a Dreamer-specific mathematical simplification rooted in binary latent structure. That is a sharper and more credible contribution than a general first-use claim, and it aligns well with the current literature. citeturn39view0turn37search1turn11view0turn14view1turn14view2
